\section*{Introduction}
Using monthly data from the Kenneth R. French Data Library \parencite{FrenchLibrary}, this report studies how estimation risk, asset definition, and implementability change mean-variance portfolio choice. We begin with the 30 industry portfolios over January 1980 to December 2025, compare sample frontiers with Bayes-Stein shrinkage variants, then test whether the main conclusions survive when the opportunity set is changed to individual stocks, augmented with a risk-free asset, or replaced by Fama-French factors and investable ETF proxies. The final part asks whether in-sample winners persist out of sample by comparing Sharpe ratios across two contiguous subperiods and then examines whether simple constraints and shrinkage improve that persistence.

\section{Data and Empirical Framework}
Following the French data legends, the core inputs are monthly value-weighted 30-industry returns, the one-month Treasury bill rate from the Fama-French factors file, and the FF3/FF5 factor series \parencite{FrenchLibrary}. The baseline industry sample runs from 1980-01 to 2025-12. When industries are compared with individual stocks or ETF proxies, each exercise uses the common date overlap so that frontier differences are not driven by mismatched windows. The stock and ETF price histories used for those comparisons were downloaded from Yahoo Finance \parencite{YahooFinance}. For the stock exercise, each stock is mapped to an FF30 industry via SIC classification and one representative stock is retained using continuity, availability, and liquidity screens.

We report the global minimum-variance (GMV) portfolio, the tangency portfolio, and the Sharpe ratio. Bayes-Stein shrinkage is used to regularize sample means toward a common target in the spirit of \textcite{jorion1986}; where stated, covariance shrinkage is added as a second layer of regularization. Because the factor portfolios are reported in excess-return form, industry and proxy returns are also converted to excess returns when they are compared with FF3 and FF5.

\section{Tasks 2--4: Industry Frontier and Opportunity Set Comparisons}
\subsection{Task 2: 30 Industry Portfolios}
Figure~\ref{fig:scope2} plots the monthly efficient frontiers for the 30 industries under three estimators: sample moments, Bayes-Stein mean shrinkage, and Bayes-Stein mean-plus-covariance shrinkage. The ordering is economically intuitive. The sample frontier is the most optimistic because it loads directly on noisy sample means, while the two shrinkage frontiers lie slightly inside it because regularization pulls the optimizer away from extreme return forecasts.

Table~\ref{tab:scope2} shows that the GMV portfolio is almost unchanged across estimators, which is expected because it depends mainly on the covariance matrix. The tangency portfolio is more sensitive because it uses expected returns directly. In this long sample the effect is modest rather than dramatic: Bayes-Stein mean shrinkage leaves tangency Sharpe almost unchanged, and adding covariance shrinkage lowers it only slightly from 0.3059 to 0.3014. The main result is therefore not that shrinkage transforms the opportunity set, but that it mildly reduces in-sample optimism without changing the qualitative ranking of feasible portfolios.

\subsection{Task 4: 30 Industries with a Risk-Free Asset}
Adding the risk-free asset changes the efficient set from the upper branch of the risky frontier to the capital market line through the tangency portfolio \parencite{Tobin1958,Sharpe1964,Lintner1965}. In economic terms, investors no longer choose among many risky efficient portfolios; they choose how much to lever or de-lever the same Sharpe-maximizing risky bundle.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/report_scope4.png}
  \caption{Industry frontier with the risk-free asset added, 1980-01 to 2025-12. The capital market line is tangent to the risky frontier at the tangency portfolio, making that portfolio the relevant risky benchmark once borrowing and lending are allowed.}
  \label{fig:scope4}
\end{figure}

Relative to Task 2, the main implication is that the tangency portfolio becomes the only relevant risky benchmark once borrowing and lending at the average sample risk-free rate are allowed. The risky-only GMV portfolio still exists, but it is no longer the key object for efficient allocation.

\section{Task 7: In-Sample vs Out-of-Sample Persistence}
The persistence test splits the sample into two contiguous periods, 1980-01 to 2002-12 and 2003-01 to 2025-12, and evaluates the GMV and tangency portfolios chosen in the first period. Table~\ref{tab:scope7} reports in-sample and out-of-sample Sharpe ratios together with Jobson-Korkie and Ledoit-Wolf tests for equality of Sharpe ratios \parencite{JobsonKorkie1981,LedoitWolf2008}. Ledoit-Wolf is treated as the primary decision rule because it is more robust; Jobson-Korkie is reported as a classical cross-check.

The answer to the brief is clear. The in-sample tangency portfolios do not remain efficient out of sample in any strong sense: both 30-industry TAN and FF5 TAN suffer statistically significant Sharpe deterioration under Ledoit-Wolf. By contrast, 30-industry GMV does not show significant deterioration, and FF5 GMV is borderline, with Jobson-Korkie rejecting but Ledoit-Wolf not rejecting at the 5\% level. That disagreement is not especially surprising: Jobson-Korkie is more dependent on iid normal-return approximations, whereas Ledoit-Wolf is designed to be more robust when those assumptions are doubtful \parencite{JobsonKorkie1981,LedoitWolf2008}. Persistence is therefore much stronger for conservative low-risk portfolios than for high-Sharpe in-sample tangency picks.

\section{Task 8 Extension: Can Constraints and Shrinkage Improve Persistence?}
From an investment-management perspective, Task 8 matters because unconstrained mean-variance portfolios can be economically implausible even when they look attractive in sample. We therefore test whether a simple no-short-selling constraint, and then Bayes-Stein shrinkage as a cross-check, produce more credible out-of-sample behavior \parencite{jagannathan2003}.

The full-sample constraint results show why this extension is useful. For FF3, unconstrained tangency Sharpe is 0.2832, but it relies on gross leverage of 74.66; imposing $w_i \geq 0$ reduces Sharpe to 0.2387 while making the portfolio implementable. The same constraint leaves FF5 with a relatively small Sharpe loss, from 0.2972 to 0.2726, which already suggests that FF5 is structurally more stable than FF3 or Proxy5.

In the constrained run, Industries and FF5 show no significant in-sample to out-of-sample Sharpe deterioration, whereas Proxy5 still deteriorates sharply. A separate shrinkage sensitivity check points in the same direction but is weaker: for unconstrained tangency portfolios, the industry Sharpe drop improves only modestly from -0.2560 under the sample estimator to -0.2344 under joint mean-and-covariance shrinkage, while FF5 changes very little across shrinkage variants. The extension therefore supports a narrow conclusion: simple constraints do more to improve persistence than shrinkage alone, and the benefit is strongest in the universes that were already less dependent on extreme leverage.

\section{Conclusion}
Three findings summarize the report. First, shrinkage moderates in-sample optimism, but in a long sample its effect on frontier shape is modest rather than transformative. Second, sample alignment matters materially: the industry-versus-stock result changes far more with the matched window than with the estimator. Third, constrained portfolios are more credible than unconstrained tangency portfolios when persistence is the objective, with the strongest evidence coming from industries and FF5.
